# Introduction
 Hello and welcome to Week Eight of DES250: Designing with Data! This week was all about sharing our progress in groups through slideshow progress reports in a structured critique format, then pairing up with someone from a different group to develop a design proposition in response to each other's projects as well as continuing to develop our data portraits through the independent study.

### Progress Reports
 I was away for this session, but getting peer feedback at this stage felt too important to skip. I checked in with Leo first to make sure it was okay to arrange an alternative, and once he confirmed it was fine I asked Ori if he'd be willing to present my slideshow on my behalf, which he every so kindly agreed to do!

 The three feedback questions I'd added into my slideshow were:
 1. Is it too abstract without any text on screen?
 2. Are the two datasets too disconnected?
 3. Any immediate thoughts or reactions?

 I wanted to ask these questions because they reflect the core problems I'm still working through in my project. I keep wondering whether the piece can carry meaning on its own without any explanatory text, or whether the abstraction, when pushed too far could transform into obscurity. I also wanted to know about whether combining whale shark data from the Gulf of Mexico with plankton data from the Northern Sea reads as a coherent conceptual choice or just feels disconnected. The third question was intentionally open ended. I wanted to see whether comments like "cool" or "interesting" would come up when people were just asked for their immediate reaction, because if they do, it would let me know that the visual style of the work is achieveing what I want it to, which is to be visually engaging. I wanted to have some confirmation that the data portrait is being received the way I intended. As it would reinforce that the viusal direction and my choice to present the data in this way works.

 Ori relayed the following feedback I recieved:
 - The work looks visually strong and engaging.
 - The dataset still isn't immediately readable, viewers aren't sure what they're looking at.
 - The concept and the visual output feel well matched.
 - The data could be made clearer.

 From the feedback, I can tell that the work is visually compelling but the data isn't legible on its own. What's interesting is that this directly answers my first question: yes, without text it is too abstract for most viewers to identify the source material. But I don't think the solution is necessarily to make the data more literal. The feedback that the concept and output are well matched tells me the visual choice of representation is working and that the issue is more about whether there's a way to give viewers just enough context to orient themselves without over explaining the piece and losing the ambiguity that makes it interesting to look at.
 
### Critical Design Propositions
 I didn't have a formal partner for this session, but I managed to catch Ori after class and we spent some time walking each other through our projects and the feedback we'd each received, which ended up being a really useful conversation even outside of the structured format. Ori's project is about translating a person's tone when they communicate through online messaging platforms, essentially trying to make the emotional register of a message visible in a way that text alone doesn't convey.

 Feedback I Gave Ori:
 - After hearing him talk through it, the question I kept coming back to was: are you trying to totally rewrite the social cues we already have when communicating online, or are you trying to make the existing ones clearer? Because those are actually two very different projects. One is about replacing a system, the other is about augmenting it and the answer shapes everything from the visual language to the intended audience to the kind of impact the work can have. Ori said it was useful feedback that gave him something more concrete to think through, which was good to hear.

 Feedback I Received:
 - He suggested that leaning into a bluer colour palette, as I am currently using orange and purple and incorporating more wave like forms into the visual elements could help ground the work in its source material, making the ocean connection more felt without spelling it out explicitly.

 What I find interesting about Ori's feedback is that it's not asking me to make the data more readable, it's asking me to make the feeling of the ocean more present. A bluer palette and wave like forms wouldn't tell you what you're looking at, but they might make you feel like you're somewhere near water without. This is a direction that would be interesting to look into as feedback keeps pointing to the work being visually strong but disconnected from its source material, however I am not sure how aesthetically pleasing it would look.

## Independent Study
### Reflective Summary
 From the feedback recieved, it gave me something I needed to address in my project: being more specific about what the viewer is actually looking at without making the work literal. What the feedback did do was push my thinking in a new direction. I started thinking about things that are alive in the ocean that are naturally abstract looking which got me to microorganisms and bacteria. They are colourful, circular, have really interesting patterns and designs, and there is something about them that is abstract but still instantly recognisable as a biological thing. If the visual language of the work pulled from that world rather than from conventional ocean imagery like waves or fish, it might give viewers a way into the piece without me having to label everything. The thing I am happy about from the feedback is that everyone said it looked nice or interesting. That matters because one of the core goals of this project is making something that catches your attention and keeps it, something that stays in your mind, so you are more likely to come back to it and think about it. I want to follow up on the microorganism and bacteria idea and see if leaning into that visual language helps make the work more connectable for viewers.

### Project Development 
 The most significant change I worked on was the visual theme of the data portrait. I wanted to expand on the thinking from my reflective summary and actually test whether basing the visual language on ocean microorganisms and bacteria would make the work feel more connected to its source material than the previous version did. I started with the background, switching from the warm pale blue of the earlier version to a deep black to make it seem like the shapes were glowing. Beyond the background, I redesigned the form language of the organisms themselves. The previous version used simple circles with dissolving radial gradients to create soft and formless shapes. The new version introduces much more biological specificity with elongated elliptical bodies that rotate as they drift and fine hair like appendages radiating outward like cilia, smaller spherical cells clustering around larger forms, glowing cores that pulse with light. Each element is still driven by the data, using position from GPS coordinates, size and pulse rate from movement speed but the visual represents a much more biological visual theme. I also made changes in the colour palette from Klimt inspired aquas and sage greens to bright and neon greens, yellows, oranges and blues and bright glowing whites, inspired by the bioluminescence in deep sea creatures. This version gives the data a visual home that actually makes sense for what it is. The whale shark movement data produces the larger, more dramatic organisms and the plankton data produces the smaller clustered forms scattered across the canvas. They feel like they belong together, the way they actually do in the ocean by creating one big portrait of the ocean, showing what is invisible to most people.
