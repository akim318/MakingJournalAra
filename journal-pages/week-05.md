## Reflective Proposal 

Across the first four weeks of this course, a consistent theme ran through my experiments: tracking data that happens quietly in the background of ordinary life, unnoticed by the people generating it. In Week 1, I tracked my own work tasks and found the data collected itself as a byproduct of simply doing my job. In Week 2, p5.js and vibe coding showed me that digital tools could make invisible routines visible in interactive ways. The experiment that clarified my direction most was the Week 3 live data visualisation using Atlantic puffin migration data. Animating thirteen birds across years of migration felt less like a technical exercise and more like an act of witness. Something vast and continuous was happening beyond everyday human awareness, and mapping it brought it into view.

Week 4 readings sharpened my thinking. D'Ignazio and Klein argue in Data Feminism that choices about what to show and what to leave out are never neutral, and Mikaere frames data collection as a political act. My own conclusion runs counter to the reductive reading: I don't think seeing something as data diminishes it. It might be the most honest way of seeing it at all. If you break life down to its fundamentals, it is pure data. We are data and our data tells our story, not stripping things of meaning, but stripping away bias. Representing data is therefore never decorative, it is an argument about what that data means and who it is for.

Off the southern tip of Sri Lanka, a blue whale population shares its habitat with one of the world's busiest shipping lanes, facing 200 vessels per day. Their tracking data collected for years. The problem was never missing information, it was that nobody was paying attention in the right way. When MSC Group rerouted its vessels in 2022, simulations suggested the change could reduce fatal ship strikes by up to 95 percent. The gap was not technological but human. People did not feel connected to it.

My thematic focus is that gap between data existing and people caring. I am drawn to a near future scenario in which real time ocean biotelemetry becomes ambient public culture, present in homes, galleries, and public spaces as something people live with, not just consult. Movebank will supply live animal tracking for seabirds and marine mammals; ICES will provide oceanographic measurements including sea surface temperature. Together they form a portrait of a living system under pressure. The technical implementation will develop iteratively through experimentation.

The final artefact will be a slowly shifting, screen based colour field visualisation, a living painting driven by real data. Animal movement and environmental conditions will translate directly into colour, saturation, and flow, changing continuously in real time. No map, no legend, no chart, just the ocean breathing. The choice of beauty as the mode of representation is itself the critical argument: if data is the most honest version of life, it deserves to be seen that way. Something functional can be dismissed. Something you would hang on your wall and return to cannot. The question for a public audience is not whether they understand the data, it is whether they feel it. Because feeling it is what changes behaviour. The researchers had the data, what the whales needed was for the right people to care.
